**1. I-Go-You-Go (Player actions all dice, then turn ends):**
*   **Pros:**
    *   **Simpler Tracking:** Easier to remember whose turn it is and who has acted.
    *   **Grand Planning:** Allows a player to execute complex, multi-unit strategies and combos in a single, uninterrupted burst. You can coordinate attacks, movements, and merges without immediate counter-play.
    *   **Feeling of Power:** When it's your turn, you feel like you're commanding your whole army.
*   **Cons:**
    *   **Player Downtime:** Can lead to significant downtime for the non-active player, especially with 8 units on the board. This can make the game feel slow or disengaging.
    *   **Alpha Strike Potential:** The active player can potentially launch a devastating, encountered assault, potentially eliminating multiple enemy units before the opponent gets to react.
    *   **Less Reactive:** The game becomes more about executing pre-planned moves than reacting to the opponent's immediate actions.
    *   **Fog of War Impact:** Under FoW, if I move one unit to reveal an enemy, I can then immediately activate *all* my other units swarm it without your units getting a chance to react first. This can lead to very sudden eliminations.

**2. Alternating Activations (Player actions 1 dice, then turn ends):**
*   **Pros:**
    *   **Reduced Downtime:** Players are constantly engaged, as turns are quick and back-and-forth.
    *   **Increased Reactivity:** Every unit activation is a mini-decision that forces the opponent to immediately consider their counter-move. This creates a dynamic, back-and-forth tactical dance.
    *   **More Tension:** You commit one unit at a time, making each choice feel more impactful as you wait to see how your opponent responds.
    *   **Fairer Combat:** If I attack your unit and fail, you get to activate one of *your* units immediately, perhaps to retreat or reinforce, before I can bring another attacker to bear.
    *   **Fog of War Impact:** You reveal an enemy with one unit, but then your opponent gets to react with *their* unit before you can commit more forces. This makes FoW discoveries more tense and balanced.
*   **Cons:**
    *   **More Complex Tracking:** Requires a mechanism to track which units have activated in the current round (e.g., tokens, rotating a dial on the base of each unit).
    *   **Less "Grand" Planning:** It's harder to execute multi-step combos across several units without interruption.
    *   **Potential for "Ping-Pong":** Can sometimes feel like players are just trading single activations without deeper strategy, though good unit design mitigates this.</expert>
