## Hex Dice Tactics Rules Review: Expert Collaboration

This review synthesizes the feedback from experienced game designers on the "Hex Dice Tactics" board game rules (v1.0 & v1.2). The game presents an interesting tactical concept using dice as units on a hexagonal grid with deterministic combat.

**Overall Concept:** The core idea of using standard six-sided dice where the face value determines the unit type, stats, and movement is innovative and offers an intuitive way to manage diverse units. The shift to a hex grid and deterministic combat provides a foundation for strategic positioning and planning. The Merging mechanic is a notable strength, adding strategic depth and player agency.

**Key Areas of Feedback & Analysis:**

1.  **Setup & Army Composition (v1.0 & v1.2):**
    *   **Strength:** The concept of rolling your army is unique.
    *   **Area for Improvement:** The primary concern is the *extreme* randomness of the initial army composition. Rolling your entire starting force can lead to highly unbalanced armies from the outset, where one player might be severely disadvantaged by bad luck before the game even truly begins. The 1/3 reroll option provides minimal mitigation for small army sizes.
    *   **Recommendation:** Consider alternative methods for army generation that introduce more player control or less setup variance. Options could include a limited draft system, a point-buy system after rolling, or a larger initial pool of dice rolled from which players select their starting army. Deployment zone descriptions, especially on the R=8 map, could benefit from clearer visual or textual definitions of the "adjacent hexes pointing towards the center."

2.  **Dice Soldiers (Unit Types) & Movement (v1.0):**
    *   **Strength:** Tying stats directly to dice value is clean and easy to reference. Unique movement patterns provide strategic depth.
    *   **Area for Improvement:** Some movement descriptions could be clearer using more precise hex-grid terminology. D4's movement (2 vertical, 1 horizontal skip) and D3's "fixed distance 3 steps" L-move are less common than standard hex adaptations of chess moves and need careful diagramming or description. D1 having Range 4 is quite long for a basic unit. D6's "0 movement" rule contradicts its "Special Attack" which allows it to move into the target hex on a successful hit; clarify if this attack is an exception to its movement rule or if it's true movement is conditional on winning combat. The D6 adjacent armor bonus (+1) seems statistically weak for its immobility.
    *   **Recommendation:** Refine movement descriptions using clear hex-grid language (e.g., referencing hex 'sides' or 'axes' consistently). Ensure diagrams perfectly match descriptions. Clarify the D6's movement ability relative to its Special Attack. Review the balance of movement ranges and stats across all unit types.

3.  **Actions & Combat (v1.0):**
    *   **Strength:** A clear set of actions (Move, Reroll, Guard, Ranged, Special, Merging). Deterministic combat removes luck and focuses on tactical positioning and unit match-ups. The Armor Reduction mechanic is a strategic element that rewards focusing fire and punishes defensive units over multiple engagements.
    *   **Area for Improvement:** Spending an entire turn on Reroll or Guard actions feels high-cost unless the tactical situation is critical. The Armor Reduction mechanic, while strategic, could potentially lead to slower combat resolution if players aren't efficiently focusing attacks. The duration of Armor reduction (appears permanent) is clear, as is the effect of reaching 0 Armor. Tracking reduction with tokens is a good component choice.
    *   **Recommendation:** The combat system is solid. Players will adapt to the cost of Reroll/Guard based on perceived value; testing will show if these actions feel worthwhile compared to moving/attacking.

4.  **Merging (v1.1):**
    *   **Strength:** This is a significant strategic addition that allows players to adapt their army composition during the game, mitigate bad initial rolls, and create powerful units.     *   **Area for Improvement:** The rule allowing the resulting unit an immediate action "if the Target Unit did not take action last turn" is confusingly worded.
